The Water Cycle

The water cycle, also known as the hydrological cycle, describes the continuous movement of water on, above, and below the surface of the Earth. This cycle has no starting or ending point — it is a continuous loop driven primarily by solar energy and gravity.

Evaporation is the process by which liquid water from oceans, lakes, and rivers is converted into water vapor and rises into the atmosphere. The sun provides the heat energy needed for this transformation. Transpiration from plants also contributes significant amounts of water vapor, and together with evaporation, the combined process is called evapotranspiration.

As water vapor rises into the cooler upper atmosphere, it undergoes condensation — changing from a gas back into tiny liquid droplets. These droplets cluster around tiny particles of dust and salt to form clouds. When the droplets combine and grow heavy enough, they fall back to Earth as precipitation in the form of rain, snow, sleet, or hail.

Once precipitation reaches the ground, it follows several paths. Some water flows across the surface as runoff, eventually collecting in streams, rivers, and ultimately the ocean. Other water seeps into the soil through a process called infiltration. This groundwater can be stored in underground aquifers for thousands of years, be absorbed by plant roots, or slowly discharge into streams and oceans.

The water cycle plays a critical role in regulating Earth's climate and distributing freshwater resources. Human activities such as deforestation, urbanization, and climate change are altering the natural water cycle, affecting precipitation patterns and water availability around the world.
